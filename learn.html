Kidney Stones

Definiton : Kidney stones are small mineral deposits that form inside your kidneys. Passing stones larger than 5ml is often excessively painful, however rarely leads to permanent damage. In extreme cases surgery may be needed, but  pain medication and plenty of water is often enough.

Symptoms


• Urinating more often than usual 
• Constant need to urinate
• Urinating only small amounts
• Pain during urination
• Pain that comes in waves and changing in intensity
• Sharp pain in the lower side and back, below the ribs
• Pain in the lower abdomen
• Pink, red or brown urine
• Cloudy or foul-smelling urine
• Nausea and vomiting
• Fever and chills with infection

Prevention


• Drink water throughout the day. (2.5 liters) of urine a day.

• Eat less oxalate-rich food. If you tend to form calcium oxalate stones, your doctor may recommend restricting foods rich in oxalates. These include beets, spinach, sweet potatoes, nuts, tea, chocolate and soy products.

• Eat less salt and animal protein. Reduce the amount of salt you eat and try legumes as an alternative protein source.

Medications by Disease


• Calcium stones. To help prevent calcium stones from forming, you may be prescribe a thiazide diuretic or a phosphate-containing preparation.

• Uric acid stones. Allopurinol (Zyloprim, Aloprim) reduces uric acid levels in your blood and urine to keep your urine alkaline.

• Struvite stones. Keep your urine free of bacteria that cause infection. Long-term use of antibiotics in small doses may help achieve this goal.

What Causes Kidney Stones?

The horrible pain of kidney stones is actually directly related to a different kind of pain, chemical equilibrium. The minerals that form these stones are produced in equilibrium reactions. Due to the nature of equilibrium shifts and Le Chatelier’s principle, kidney stone formation is dependent on ion concentrations in the urine.  

Calcium Oxalate

Calcium oxalate, a common type of kidney stone, is produced in the following equilibrium reaction. 

The reversible reaction of solid calcium oxalate dissolving into calcium ions and oxalic acid.

Calcium not used in the bones and muscles go to the kidney to be flushed out in the urine. Oxalate is from waste materials. A diet of calcium or oxalic acid rich foods, affects the ion concentrations in the kidneys. According to Le Chatelier’s principle and the common ion effect this shifts the reaction to the left towards the precipitate. 

However a low calcium diet is not recommended either. Low levels of calcium cause more oxalate to be adsorbed during digestion, this increase in oxalate ions promotes kidney stones.

Prevention

                                     By consuming the right amount of calcium in your diet, and drinking plenty of water (lowers concentration of ions in urine due to greater volume), you may never experience kidney stones!
